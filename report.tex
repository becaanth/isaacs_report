\section{Background}

\subsection{Pursuit-Evasion Games}

Pursuit-evasion games study the interaction between one or more pursuers and one or more evaders moving in a domain $\mathcal{D} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$. 
The objective of the pursuers is to capture the evaders, while the evaders aim to avoid capture. 
These problems arise in robotics, control theory, and differential games.

A common formulation models the players as dynamical systems. 
For a single pursuer with state $x_p(t)$ and evader with state $x_e(t)$, their dynamics may be written as
\[
\dot{x}_p(t) = u_p(t), \quad \dot{x}_e(t) = u_e(t),
\]
where $u_p(t), u_e(t) \in \mathcal{U}$ are control inputs subject to bounded speed constraints, e.g., $\|u_p\| \leq v_p$, $\|u_e\| \leq v_e$. The game terminates upon capture, often defined as
\[
\|x_p(t) - x_e(t)\| \leq \varepsilon,
\]
for some radius $\varepsilon > 0$.

The pursuer seeks a control strategy $u_p(\cdot)$ that guarantees capture in finite time, while the evader seeks to maximize the capture time or avoid capture indefinitely. 
Strategies may be open-loop or feedback, and optimal strategies are often characterized using tools from differential game theory, such as Hamilton--Jacobi--Isaacs (HJI) equations.
In higher dimensional spaces, ore even with more complex kinematics, solutions to these equations are often intractable to compute.

Pursuit--evasion problems are closely related to motion planning under uncertainty and adversarial conditions. 
Sampling-based planners are often adapted to compute strategies or reachable sets in high-dimensional spaces, particularly when the evader's behavior is only partially known~\cite{}.

\subsection{The N-Pursuer Visibility-Based Pursuit-Evasion Game}

In the N-pursuer visibility-based pursuit-evasion problem, a team of $N$ pursuers cooperatively searches for an evader with an unknown position in a known environment $\mathcal{D} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$.
We denote the configuration space of the environment, exclusive of obstacles, as $\mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{D}$.
One key distinction from the standard model of the pursuit-evasion game is that information is limited by line-of-sight visibility: the evader is detected only if it lies within the visible region of at least one pursuer.
This visible region is defined by the joint configurations of the pursuer, the shape of $\mathcal{D}$, and the position of obstacles.

Let the pursuers have states $\{x_i(t)\}_{i=1}^N \subset \mathcal{C}$. The environment induces a visibility map: for a pursuer at $x_i$, its visibility region is
\[
\mathcal{V}(x_i) = \{ y \in \mathcal{D} \mid \text{$y$ is visible from $x_i$ (line-of-sight not obstructed)} \}.
\]
In this variation of the pursuit evasion-game, the evader is often treated as an abstraction rather than a physical entity. 
We assume the position of the pursuer $e(t) \in \mathcal{C}$ at time $t$ is a continuous function $e:[0,\infty) \to\mathcal{C}$ that describes the path of the evader moving at arbitrary speeds. 
This allows the evader to inhabit any location in a connected subspace $\mathcal{S}\in \mathcal{C}\\ \mathcal{V}(x)$. We refer to $\mathcal{S}(t)$ as the shadow set at time $t$. 

Unlike classical pursuit-evasion, the goal is not immediate capture of the evader but guaranteed detection. 
The pursuers seek strategies that ensure that, regardless of the evader trajectory, the evader will eventually be observed:
\[
\exists\, T < \infty \ \text{such that} \ e(T) \in \bigcup_{i=1}^N \mathcal{V}(x_i(T)).
\]

A central concept is the partition of $\mathcal{C}$ into cleared and contaminated regions. 
A point $y \in \mathcal{D}$ is cleared at time $t$ if it has been visible to some pursuer at some prior time and cannot be re-entered by the evader without being detected. 
This is equivalent to it not being path-connected to a contaminated region.
Otherwise, it is contaminated. Let $\mathcal{\tilde{S}}(t)$ and $\mathcal{\bar{S}}(t)$ denote the cleared and contaminated regions, respectively, with
\[
\mathcal{C} = \mathcal{\tilde{S}}(t) \cup \mathcal{\bar{S}}(t).
\]
Pursuer strategies must ensure that $\mathcal{\bar{S}}(t)$ shrinks monotonically, avoiding recontamination.
This effectively collapses to a constrained form of the frontier expansion problem where frontiers are dynamic and may rejoin each other.

A common abstraction decomposes $\mathcal{C}$ into a cell complex or graph $G = (V,E)$, where vertices represent regions and edges represent adjacency.
Visibility constraints induce a notion of guarded vertices~\cite{}. 
The problem reduces to finding a sequence of moves for $N$ pursuers such that all vertices become cleared without allowing recontamination, analogous to node-searching problems on graphs~\cite{}.

The visibility-based pursuit-evasion problem is significantly harder than classical pursuit-evasion due to partial information and the need for coordination. 
Determining the minimum $N$ required for guaranteed detection is generally NP-hard~\cite{}. 
This leads to many discretizations, sampling-based methods and learning approaches to the problem.

\subsection{Shadow Events}
Central to some algorithms in the literature is the concept of shadow events~\cite{bibid}. 
This framework provides a conceptual analog of pursuer movement that neatly fits into the N-Pursuer Visibility-Based Pursuit-Evasion game.
Briefly, shadow events describe a framework for propagating labels assigned to particular unseen regions in a space based on a hierarchy of dominance under common interactons.

Introduced in \cite{}, shadow events are primarily used to keep track of contaminated areas in the game's space of interest.
As such, the labels of concern are whether an area is clear or contaminated.
Naturally, contaminated labels dominate cleared labels, as when a shadow we have cleared intersects with an unchecked shadow, the evading agent may move into the previously check space.

Five main shadow events are introduced. These are
\begin{itemize}
	\item Appear - A shadow is instantiated when a prviously seen part of the environment becomes hidden.
	\item Disappear - Existing shadows vanish when the come into the visibility cone of the pursuers.
	\item Merge - Two or more existing shadows join together and adopt the same shadow label when a shadow appears linking them together.
	\item Split - One shadow can be divided into multiple shadows when the pursuers visibility cones cause them to be non-path connected in 4-neighbour space.
\end{itemize}
In practice these events are calculated on a continuous space by discretizing into a grid, calculating shadows based on line of sight from agent positions in a joint configurations, and propagating dominant labels to the 4-neighbours of any unseen cell. 

Before desribing the process of propogating labels, we begin with the following definition. 
A shadow is clear at a time $t$ if it is not possible for an evader, as defined earlier, to inhabit the shadow. 
Otherwise, a shadow is contaminated.

We may now state the relationship between the shadow events and labels as
\begin{itemize}
	\item Appear - A shadow appearing from previously visible areas is marked clear.
	\item Disappear - Labels are discarded
	\item Merge - When shadows merge, the dominant label is assigned to all of the shadows. That is to say, if any of the merging shadows is contaminated,the resulting shadow is contaminated, and otherwise it is clear.
	\item Split - All shadows resulting from a split inherit the label of the original shadow.
\end{itemize}

Of course, algorithms may use this information in their planning loop, and several considered implementations do so~\cite{bibid}.
The primary drawback of this approach is the extreme cost of computing the shadow label propagation. 
in a 64mx64m environment at a resolution of 1m, discretization yields 4096 cells, each of which has to be check for line of sight from $N$ positions.
Every resulting shadow position then must be checked for neighbours, resulting in a breadth first search through the graph for all connected shadows.
This leads to significant slow-downs in sampling on more complex and larger maps.
Speed-ups can be made by decreasing the resolution, but this comes at the cost of potentially missing contaminations.

Nonetheless, this concept provides a simple way to monitor and evaluate approaches to this problem.
As such we find it useful to utilize shadow labels as a metric in the evaluation of the various algorithms detailed below.
