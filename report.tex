\begin{abstract}
	The study of planning under adversarial conditions has become of great interest as the adoption of robotics for security, defense, and surveillance increases. 
	Further, adversarial planning can be used to account for worst-case scenarios in critical areas, such as risk-aware motion planning or search and rescue.
	Often, this study is conducted under the framework of differential game theory, a methodology that studies optimal outcomes of scenarios with multiple rational actors that evolve according to differential equations.
    One such scenario is the $N$-pursuer visibility-based pursuit evasion game, in which a team of pursuers attempts to examine an entire area in such a way that a moving evader would be unable to escape detection.
	This game has been used to model environmental inspection, perimeter defense, and search and rescue problems, but most approaches tend to only evaluate on simple scenarios and against similar approaches.
	In this report, we rectify this gap through the study and comparison the quantitative and qualitative results associated with several state of the art approaches.
\end{abstract}

Adversarial games in continuous spaces have long been studied under the field of differential game theory, developed by Rufus Isaac's in the 1950s~\cite{}.
These games, contrary to their discrete classical game theoretical counterpoints, have the evolution of the game evolve according to differential equations.
Differential games have been used in several application spaces, such as perimeter defense, risk-averse landing path plannign for airplanes, and ... \cite{}.
In robotics, differential games find many applications in military ...

Recently, more interest has been taken in multi-agent differential games~\cite{}. 
These, of course, model more realistic scenarios and engagements. 
However, they must often settle for sub-optimal solutions due to the curse of dimensionality and the difficulty of somputing Hamilton-Jacobi-Isaacs solutions.~\cite{}

One such multi-agent game is the multi-pursuer vision-based pursuit evasion game.
 In this formulation, the evader, or evaders, are often treated as abstract entities that can move at arbitrary speeds. 
 This forces the pursuing team to plan for the worst-case scenario.
 As such, the problem devolves into a type of constrained frontier expansion problem on a known path.
 This makes the formulation of interest to motion planning researchers.
 
Many solutions have been proposed for this problem, from learning-based methods, sampling-based approaches, and graph-search algorithms~\cite{}.
However, in the literature, practitioners tend to compare only against solutions they have readily available - ignoring suitable baselines from other problem formulations and only evaluating on limited subsets of the total problem space.
As such, the state of the art is lacking in honest analysis and comparison of different approaches to multi-agent differential games.

Following the growth of popularity of game theory, an effort to evaluate the performance of different strategies was undertaken on Axelrod's tournament - a contest in which different policies were pitted against each other in varying conditions to compare outcomes.
Here, we pursue a simplified version of Axelrod's tournament on one differential game.
We implement four approaches to the multi-pursuer observability-based pursuit evasion game, and compare them on numerous scenarios using common metrics.
We identify and analyse the behaviours of each method under various conditions that challenge their formulations, and use the information to recommend steps for future works.

\section{Background}

\subsection{Pursuit-Evasion Games}

Pursuit-evasion games study the interaction between one or more pursuers and one or more evaders moving in a domain $\mathcal{D} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$. 
The objective of the pursuers is to capture the evaders, while the evaders aim to avoid capture. 
These problems arise in robotics, control theory, and differential games.

A common formulation models the players as dynamical systems. 
For a single pursuer with state $x_p(t)$ and evader with state $x_e(t)$, their dynamics may be written as
\[
\dot{x}_p(t) = u_p(t), \quad \dot{x}_e(t) = u_e(t),
\]
where $u_p(t), u_e(t) \in \mathcal{U}$ are control inputs subject to bounded speed constraints, e.g., $\|u_p\| \leq v_p$, $\|u_e\| \leq v_e$. The game terminates upon capture, often defined as
\[
\|x_p(t) - x_e(t)\| \leq \varepsilon,
\]
for some radius $\varepsilon > 0$.

The pursuer seeks a control strategy $u_p(\cdot)$ that guarantees capture in finite time, while the evader seeks to maximize the capture time or avoid capture indefinitely. 
Strategies may be open-loop or feedback, and optimal strategies are often characterized using tools from differential game theory, such as Hamilton--Jacobi--Isaacs (HJI) equations.
In higher dimensional spaces, ore even with more complex kinematics, solutions to these equations are often intractable to compute.

Pursuit--evasion problems are closely related to motion planning under uncertainty and adversarial conditions. 
Sampling-based planners are often adapted to compute strategies or reachable sets in high-dimensional spaces, particularly when the evader's behavior is only partially known~\cite{}.

\subsection{The N-Pursuer Visibility-Based Pursuit-Evasion Game}

In the N-pursuer visibility-based pursuit-evasion problem, a team of $N$ pursuers cooperatively searches for an evader with an unknown position in a known environment $\mathcal{D} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$.
We denote the configuration space of the environment, exclusive of obstacles, as $\mathcal{C} \subset \mathcal{D}$.
One key distinction from the standard model of the pursuit-evasion game is that information is limited by line-of-sight visibility: the evader is detected only if it lies within the visible region of at least one pursuer.
This visible region is defined by the joint configurations of the pursuer, the shape of $\mathcal{D}$, and the position of obstacles.

Let the pursuers have states $\{x_i(t)\}_{i=1}^N \subset \mathcal{C}$. The environment induces a visibility map: for a pursuer at $x_i$, its visibility region is
\[
\mathcal{V}(x_i) = \{ y \in \mathcal{D} \mid \text{$y$ is visible from $x_i$ (line-of-sight not obstructed)} \}.
\]
In this variation of the pursuit evasion-game, the evader is often treated as an abstraction rather than a physical entity. 
We assume the position of the pursuer $e(t) \in \mathcal{C}$ at time $t$ is a continuous function $e:[0,\infty) \to\mathcal{C}$ that describes the path of the evader moving at arbitrary speeds. 
This allows the evader to inhabit any location in a connected subspace $\mathcal{S}\in \mathcal{C}\\ \mathcal{V}(x)$. We refer to $\mathcal{S}(t)$ as the shadow set at time $t$. 

Unlike classical pursuit-evasion, the goal is not immediate capture of the evader but guaranteed detection. 
The pursuers seek strategies that ensure that, regardless of the evader trajectory, the evader will eventually be observed:
\[
\exists\, T < \infty \ \text{such that} \ e(T) \in \bigcup_{i=1}^N \mathcal{V}(x_i(T)).
\]

A central concept is the partition of $\mathcal{C}$ into cleared and contaminated regions. 
A point $y \in \mathcal{D}$ is cleared at time $t$ if it has been visible to some pursuer at some prior time and cannot be re-entered by the evader without being detected. 
This is equivalent to it not being path-connected to a contaminated region.
Otherwise, it is contaminated. Let $\mathcal{\tilde{S}}(t)$ and $\mathcal{\bar{S}}(t)$ denote the cleared and contaminated regions, respectively, with
\[
\mathcal{C} = \mathcal{\tilde{S}}(t) \cup \mathcal{\bar{S}}(t).
\]
Pursuer strategies must ensure that $\mathcal{\bar{S}}(t)$ shrinks monotonically, avoiding recontamination.
This effectively collapses to a constrained form of the frontier expansion problem where frontiers are dynamic and may rejoin each other.

A common abstraction decomposes $\mathcal{C}$ into a cell complex or graph $G = (V,E)$, where vertices represent regions and edges represent adjacency.
Visibility constraints induce a notion of guarded vertices~\cite{}. 
The problem reduces to finding a sequence of moves for $N$ pursuers such that all vertices become cleared without allowing recontamination, analogous to node-searching problems on graphs~\cite{}.

The visibility-based pursuit-evasion problem is significantly harder than classical pursuit-evasion due to partial information and the need for coordination. 
Determining the minimum $N$ required for guaranteed detection is generally NP-hard~\cite{}. 
This leads to many discretizations, sampling-based methods and learning approaches to the problem.

\subsection{Shadow Events}
Central to some algorithms in the literature is the concept of shadow events~\cite{bibid}. 
This framework provides a conceptual analog of pursuer movement that neatly fits into the N-Pursuer Visibility-Based Pursuit-Evasion game.
Briefly, shadow events describe a framework for propagating labels assigned to particular unseen regions in a space based on a hierarchy of dominance under common interactons.

Introduced in \cite{}, shadow events are primarily used to keep track of contaminated areas in the game's space of interest.
As such, the labels of concern are whether an area is clear or contaminated.
Naturally, contaminated labels dominate cleared labels, as when a shadow we have cleared intersects with an unchecked shadow, the evading agent may move into the previously check space.

Five main shadow events are introduced. These are
\begin{itemize}
	\item Appear - A shadow is instantiated when a prviously seen part of the environment becomes hidden.
	\item Disappear - Existing shadows vanish when the come into the visibility cone of the pursuers.
	\item Merge - Two or more existing shadows join together and adopt the same shadow label when a shadow appears linking them together.
	\item Split - One shadow can be divided into multiple shadows when the pursuers visibility cones cause them to be non-path connected in 4-neighbour space.
\end{itemize}
In practice these events are calculated on a continuous space by discretizing into a grid, calculating shadows based on line of sight from agent positions in a joint configurations, and propagating dominant labels to the 4-neighbours of any unseen cell. 

Before desribing the process of propogating labels, we begin with the following definition. 
A shadow is clear at a time $t$ if it is not possible for an evader, as defined earlier, to inhabit the shadow. 
Otherwise, a shadow is contaminated.

We may now state the relationship between the shadow events and labels as
\begin{itemize}
	\item Appear - A shadow appearing from previously visible areas is marked clear.
	\item Disappear - Labels are discarded
	\item Merge - When shadows merge, the dominant label is assigned to all of the shadows. That is to say, if any of the merging shadows is contaminated,the resulting shadow is contaminated, and otherwise it is clear.
	\item Split - All shadows resulting from a split inherit the label of the original shadow.
\end{itemize}

Of course, algorithms may use this information in their planning loop, and several considered implementations do so~\cite{bibid}.
The primary drawback of this approach is the extreme cost of computing the shadow label propagation. 
in a 64mx64m environment at a resolution of 1m, discretization yields 4096 cells, each of which has to be check for line of sight from $N$ positions.
Every resulting shadow position then must be checked for neighbours, resulting in a breadth first search through the graph for all connected shadows.
This leads to significant slow-downs in sampling on more complex and larger maps.
Speed-ups can be made by decreasing the resolution, but this comes at the cost of potentially missing contaminations.

Nonetheless, this concept provides a simple way to monitor and evaluate approaches to this problem.
As such we find it useful to utilize shadow labels as a metric in the evaluation of the various algorithms detailed below.


\subsection{Probabilistic Road Maps}
The probabibilistic road map~(PRM) algorithm is a sampling-based method for motion planning. The algorithm proceeds as follows
\begin{enumerate}
	\item Sample $n$ configurations $\{q_i\}_{i=1}^n$ uniformly at random from $C_{\text{free}}$.
	\item Construct a graph $G = (V, E)$ where $V = \{q_i\}$.
	\item For each $q_i$, find its $k$-nearest neighbors $\mathcal{N}(q_i)$.
	\item For each $q_j \in \mathcal{N}(q_i)$, attempt to connect $q_i$ and $q_j$:
	\[
	E \leftarrow E \cup \{(q_i, q_j)\} \quad \text{if } \text{LocalPlanner}(q_i, q_j) \subset C_{\text{free}}.
	\]
\end{enumerate}

Given a startconfiguration $q_{\text{start}}$ and goal configuration $q_{\text{goal}}$, connect them to $G$ and search for a path using a graph search algorithm.

PRM is probabilistically complete
\[
\lim_{n \to \infty} \mathbb{P}(\text{PRM finds a path}) = 1,
\]
if a feasible path exists in $C_{\text{free}}$.

\section*{Multi-Robot Systems}

For $m$ robots, the joint configuration space is
\[
C = C_1 \times C_2 \times \cdots \times C_m,
\]
which grows exponentially with $m$.

PRM has been extended to multi-robot systems via:
\begin{itemize}
	\item \textbf{Centralized PRM:} Build a roadmap in the joint space $C$, ensuring collision avoidance between robots.
	\item \textbf{Decoupled PRM:} Plan paths independently in each $C_i$, then resolve conflicts.
	\item \textbf{PRM-based coordination graphs:} Combine individual roadmaps with synchronization constraints.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Sample-Generated Pursuit-Evasion Graphs} \label{subsec:peg}
Sample-generated pursuit-evasion graphs~(SG-PEG) utilize PRM-style sampling in a joint configuration space to find solutions to the VB-PE game.
While based on PRM algorithms, no proofs of probabilistic completeness are provided for SG-PEG.
In place of proofs, a high-level reasoning about the same properties holding is provided, though in practice, this does not carry much weight.

We begin with a description of the graph data structure utilized in SG-PEG.
The graph utilizes is a rooted di-graph with vertices representing joint robot configurations $r_{0:N}$, as well as the set of non-dominated shadows reachable by traversing the graph to the vertex in question.
For an edge to be considered valid in the context of the graph, a line in the joint configuration space $\mathcal{C}^N$ must exist that maps reachable configurations of shadow labels at the source vertex to the same at the target vertex.

The core complexity in the graph arises from the operation necessary to add an edge. 
When a new connection is established between two vertices, resulting shadow labels must be computed.
To do so, interpolation between the two associated joint configurations is utilized to obtain a number of evenly spaced poses in $\mathcal{C}^N$. 
Shadow events are then computed at these evenly spaced joint configurations, propagating the intermediate labels between the vertices.

Upon adding a new edge, the algorithm recursively searches through the neighbours of the vertex, updating the dominated labels.
This acts as bookkeeping for a solutions.
If the reachable labels are all clear for a given vertex, the vertex must denote a full clearing of the environment.
Upon this condition being met, we may then reconstruct a solution from the intermediate waypoints between the root and the cleared node, as in any tree-based motion planning method.

The authors utilize three constraints to filter which edges should be added to the graph.
The first is mutual visibility - a line of sight, or obstacle free straight line path, must exist between each pursuers configuration at vertex $i$ and its corresponding configuration at vertex $i+1$.
The second is edge-length, with a maximum distance being set between two configurations.
Finally, a minimum cycle length is set to ensure that computation is not wasted on low-information configuration changes.

Numerous sampling strategies are explored, but none seem to outperform uniform sampling in the joint configuration space.

\subsection{Visibility Roadmaps}
In \cite{}, an extension to SG-PEG is proposed. 
Several key innovations are detailed, including an improved visibility-based sampling distribution, a solution refinement method, and a process for iteratively increasing the number of pursuers until a solution can be provided.
Due to the references to visibility roadmaps in the paper title, we refer to this variant as visibility-roadmap SG-PEG~(VR-SG-PEG).
We note that, as we are interested in evaluating each detailed algorithm in sterile and identical conditions, we omit the process of increasing the number of pursuers from our method, instead keeping this fixed based on a given configuration.

In place of the uniform sampling recommended in SP-PEG, VR-SP-PEG uses an informed sampling method, referred to as web-sampling, to improve the density of samples in interesting regions.
By extension, this nominally improves convergence properties of the planner, at the cost of higher levels of computation.
The sampling proceeds in two phases: an initial visibility covering phase and an intersection sampling phase.

In the first phase, a set of initial points $\mathcal{P}\subset \mathcal{C}$ is constructed, referred to as a covering set.
This set is generated as follows: each initial point $p$ is sampled sequentially and randomly from outside the set of visible points in $\mathcal{C}$, i.e a sample $p_i$ is selected such that $p_i \notin \cup_{p_j\in \mathcal{P}}\mathcal{V}(p_j)$. This process continues until these mutually non-visible points cover the entire configuration space,$\cup_{p_j\in \mathcal{P}} \mathcal{V}(p_j) = \mathcal{C}$. 

In the second phase, a series of points, denoted by the set $\mathcal{Q}$, are sampled to expand the roadmap data structure.
This is done by sampling pairs of points $p_i, p_j \in \mathcal{P}$, denoted intersection points.
A point is then sampled uniformly from the intersection of the individual visibility cones of each point $\mathcal{V}(p_i)\cap \mathcal{V}(p_j)$, if the intersection is non-empty.
The so-called web is the union of the initial and intersection points $\mathcal{W} = \mathcal{P}\cup \mathcal{Q}$.
A separate web is sampled for each pursuer, and if a web is ever exhausted, a new set of webs is generated to continue.
The pursuit-evasion graph is constructed by sampling points from these webs without replacement.


The second innovation of note is the inclusion of a solution refinement step, devised to reduce unnecessary movement of the robots.
This acts as a post-processing step, filtering out the extraneuous plan branches.
Iteratively, two non-adjacent points from the solution $z_a, z_b$ are selected to check if a shortcut from $z_a$ to $z_b$ is still a valid solution.
In addition to standard kinematic and collision avoidance constraints, the algorithm checks whether adjusting the solution in this manner clears the same set of shadows as the original solution, using the procedure laid out in Subsection \ref{subsec:peg}.
